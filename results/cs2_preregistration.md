## predictions based on CS1 cluster structure

CS1 (Iran strikes) produced 5 named clusters. below are predictions for
which of these cluster types will appear, be absent, or be transformed
in CS2 (tariffs).

### cluster 1: "justified preemptive military action against existential threats"
**prediction: ABSENT in CS2.** this cluster's epistemic logic — existential
threat justifying preemptive force — has no direct analogue in trade policy.
however, a *structural analogue* may appear: "justified protectionist action
against existential economic threats" where tariffs are framed as necessary
defense against trade imbalance. if this appears, it is a significant finding
about the transferability of existential-threat framing across domains.

### cluster 2: "diplomatic efforts as the primary path to conflict avoidance"
**prediction: PRESENT as "negotiation as the primary path to trade resolution."**
the diplomatic-primacy frame should transfer directly — outlets that emphasized
negotiation over military action in CS1 should emphasize trade negotiation
over tariff escalation in CS2. expect geographically dispersed membership.

### cluster 3: "china's strategic gains and regional stability concerns"
**prediction: PRESENT but TRANSFORMED.** in CS1 this was about China as a
strategic observer. in CS2, China is a direct party — so this cluster should
shift from observer-realpolitik to participant-realpolitik, centering trade
balance dynamics rather than regional stability. expect stronger membership
from Asian outlets.

### cluster 4: "sovereignty and international law as central to conflict legitimacy"
**prediction: PRESENT as "free trade norms and WTO rules as central to tariff legitimacy."**
the normative-internationalist frame should transfer: outlets that invoked
international law in CS1 should invoke trade agreements and WTO dispute
resolution in CS2. expect EU and multilateral-institution-aligned outlets.

### cluster 5: "iranian resistance as justified self-defense"
**prediction: ABSENT as such, REPLACED by "targeted economies' retaliatory measures as justified self-defense."**
the self-defense-against-aggression frame should appear but with different
actors: China, EU, Canada retaliating against US tariffs rather than Iran
defending against strikes. if this structural equivalence appears, it
confirms the framework captures epistemic positions rather than event-specific
content.

### predicted NOVEL clusters in CS2 (no CS1 analogue)

1. **financial markets as primary stakeholder.** CS2 has a financial press
   layer (MarketAux data) that CS1 lacked. expect a cluster centered on
   market volatility, supply chain disruption, and corporate earnings
   impact that has no CS1 equivalent.

2. **domestic consumer impact framing.** tariffs directly affect consumer
   prices in a way military strikes do not. expect outlets (particularly
   tabloid and retail-focused press) to frame tariffs through household
   economics — cost of living, price of imports, small business survival.

3. **historical trade war analogy.** the Smoot-Hawley comparison has no
   CS1 analogue. if articles invoke 1930s protectionism, that is a novel
   epistemic frame specific to trade events.

## evaluation criteria

after CS2 analysis completes:

1. for each prediction above, score as:
   - **confirmed** — cluster appeared as predicted
   - **partially confirmed** — structural analogue appeared but differed in
     important ways (document the difference)
   - **disconfirmed** — cluster did not appear despite prediction
   - **surprised** — novel cluster appeared that was not predicted

2. transfer rate = (confirmed + partially confirmed) / total predictions

3. if transfer rate ≥ 0.60, the framework shows meaningful cross-event
   generalization. if < 0.40, the framework is event-specific and claims
   about general epistemic patterns must be withdrawn.
