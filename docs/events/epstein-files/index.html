01
Introduction

On January 30, 2026, the US Department of Justice released 3.5 million pages of investigative documents related to Jeffrey Epstein’s trafficking network. The release triggered a global media event that played out across every continent, every language community, and every political orientation. Within weeks, the Epstein files became the most written-about DOJ document release in modern history.

This report presents an epistemic map of that coverage: 6,167 articles ingested from 1,952 sources in 44 languages, with 4,155 analyzed via LLM framing extraction. Unlike conventional media analysis, we imposed no predefined categories. The system was asked a single question about each article: how does this text frame the Epstein story? The clusters that emerged from 4,155 free-text framing descriptions are the data.

The central question is not who is guilty. It is: how do different media ecosystems decide what the Epstein story is about? Is it about Prince Andrew? Political leverage? Victim justice? Scientific complicity? The answer, it turns out, depends entirely on where you read your news.

The Anglophone Dominance Problem

English accounts for 38% of all articles. Dutch (54) and Romanian (47) are the next largest—likely driven by Epstein’s connections to European elites (Prince Andrew, Peter Mandelson). Hindi coverage (35) reflects India’s tabloid ecosystem. The near-absence of Arabic (15) and Persian (16) contrasts sharply with their heavy presence in Iran strike coverage (99 and 82 respectively). The Epstein story is not “their” story.

03
Executive Summary:
Key Findings
01

The Epstein story is the most epistemically fragmented of any case study analyzed. The largest cluster accounts for only 3.6% of the corpus (Prince Andrew, 150 articles). Compare this to Ukraine 2022, where a single mega-cluster captured 49.8% of all coverage. The Epstein corpus produced 74 emergent clusters, and a full quarter of articles (25.6%) are unique singletons—frames so distinctive that no other article in the corpus shares them.

The story fractures along personality lines, not geopolitical lines. Prince Andrew (227 articles across two clusters), Ghislaine Maxwell (88), Donald Trump (63), Bill Clinton (41), and Peter Mandelson (51) each generate their own media ecosystem. Unlike war coverage, which fractures by country and ideology, scandal coverage fractures by named individual. Each name is a lens through which the entire scandal is refracted.

03

Political instrumentalization dominates over victim justice. AG Bondi’s political maneuvers (92 articles), MTG-driven political infighting (106), and transparency framing (247 combined across two clusters) together dwarf the victim/trafficking cluster (56 articles). The FILES became a political weapon. The victims—the reason the documents exist—are a footnote in global media coverage.

Media amplifies celebrity names and suppresses working scientists. The suppression index reveals a stark pattern: Danny Hillis (15 document mentions, 0 media articles), Seth Lloyd (10 docs, 0 media), Marvin Minsky (25 docs, 2 media). Meanwhile Noam Chomsky (15 docs, 127 media = 8.5x amplification) and Lawrence Summers (30 docs, 245 media = 8.2x). Media covers intellectuals the public already knows. The scientists Epstein actually funded are invisible.

The international coverage cluster (91 articles) reveals which countries consider this “their” story. Dutch (27 articles), Hindi (7), Turkish (4)—small but present. Most non-English coverage is translation or wire service pass-through, not original reporting. The Epstein scandal remains an Anglo-American media event with European satellite coverage driven by specific named individuals (Andrew for the UK, Mandelson for Labour).

25.6% of articles are singletons—the highest of any case study. This means a quarter of all coverage frames the scandal in ways no other outlet shares. Maximum epistemic diversity. Every fourth article tells the Epstein story from an angle found nowhere else in the corpus. This is what hyperfragmentation looks like: not disagreement between two sides, but 1,063 articles each telling a different story.

Index

The suppression index compares how often an individual appears in DOJ primary documents versus how often they appear in global media coverage. A ratio below 1.0 means the media covers the person less than the documents warrant. A ratio of 0.0 means the person appears in the documents but is completely invisible in media coverage. The pattern is stark: media amplifies celebrity intellectuals and suppresses working scientists.

The Pattern

Media coverage amplifies names the public already recognizes (Chomsky 8.5x, Summers 8.2x, Hawking 4.9x, Dershowitz 4.5x) while completely suppressing the working scientists Epstein actually funded and embedded in his network (Hillis 0.0x, Lloyd 0.0x, Minsky 0.1x). The suppression threshold is not about evidence—Hillis (15 doc mentions) has the same document footprint as Chomsky (15 doc mentions). One gets 127 media articles. The other gets zero. The difference is celebrity, not culpability.

Why Scandals Shatter

Ukraine 2022 had two clear “sides” and one dominant global narrative—nearly half of all articles converged on a single framing. Iran 2026 was multipolar: multiple competing framings, but several large clusters. Epstein 2026 has no “sides” at all. It has named individuals, each generating a separate media ecosystem. There is no Andrew-vs-Trump axis, no prosecution-vs-defense binary. There are 74 framings, most of them small, and a quarter of all articles standing entirely alone. This is because scandals don’t have clear geopolitical alignments—they have named individuals, institutional affiliations, and personal histories, each of which refracts the story differently.

!

The fewer clusters does not mean less diversity. Epstein has 74 clusters (fewer than Iran’s 119), yet its singleton rate is 4x higher. The lower cluster count reflects a smaller analyzable corpus (4,155 vs. 1,267), while the extreme singleton rate reflects genuine epistemic fragmentation. Each article is more likely to be telling a unique story.

Methodology
Data sources
GDELT + World News API + Reddit. 30 named-individual queries + institutional queries + angle queries across 4 time windows.
Time windows
Dec 2025 (initial reports), Jan 2026 (major DOJ release), Feb 2026 (European fallout), Mar 2026 (ongoing reverberations).
Corpus
6,167 articles ingested, 4,155 with extractable text analyzed. 1,952 unique sources, 44 languages.
Text extraction
trafilatura → newspaper3k fallback. 67% text extraction success rate.
Translation
Helsinki-NLP MarianMT (20+ pairs) → Meta NLLB-200 (200 languages). All local on nitrogen GPU.
Pass 1
Mistral-Small-24B (Q4_K_M) on RTX A4000 (nitrogen). Open-ended framing extraction with no predefined categories.
Clustering
sentence-transformers (all-MiniLM-L6-v2) → UMAP (384→15 dimensions) → HDBSCAN. 74 emergent clusters + 1,063 singletons.
Suppression index
Epstein Investigation API entity data (primary docs) cross-referenced with media mention counts from corpus.
Infrastructure
Mistral-Small-24B on RTX A4000 (nitrogen). Translation on same GPU. All inference local, no cloud APIs.
On Methodology

No predefined framing categories were used at any stage. The LLM was asked to describe each article’s framing in its own words. Clusters emerged from semantic similarity between these free-text descriptions. The 74 cluster labels above—“Prince Andrew,” “AG Bondi Political Maneuver,” “Trafficking Syndicate”—were discovered by the system, not imposed by researchers. The 1,063 singletons (25.6%) represent articles whose framing no other article in the corpus shares.

This report was produced using the NewsKaleidoscope epistemic mapping system. Corpus data sourced from GDELT, World News API, and Reddit. LLM analysis performed on local infrastructure with no data sent to external APIs. All framing categories are emergent, not predefined. The suppression index was computed by cross-referencing primary DOJ document entity mentions with media coverage frequency.
