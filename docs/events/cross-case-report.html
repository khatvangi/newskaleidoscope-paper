01
Introduction

When a military power strikes, the global media system activates. Thousands of journalists in dozens of languages attempt to explain what happened and why it matters. The words they choose, the historical context they invoke, the voices they amplify and the perspectives they silence collectively form an epistemic map: a record of how different cultures understood the same event.

This report presents a computational analysis of that map for two military conflicts: the US-Israeli strike on Iran in February 2026, and Russia's invasion of Ukraine in February 2022. Using identical methodology across both events, we extracted framing patterns from 3,363 articles without predefined categories, allowing the data to reveal its own structure.

The central question is not which framing is "correct." It is: why does the global media system converge on a single narrative for one war, and fracture into competing narratives for another?

03
Executive Summary

This analysis reveals a fundamental asymmetry in how the global media system processes different military conflicts. The findings challenge assumptions about media neutrality and demonstrate that epistemic fragmentation is itself a measurable signal of geopolitical complexity.

01

Ukraine coverage is four times more "legible" to analytical models than Iran coverage. Only 14% of Ukraine articles produced contested framing between models, compared to 57.7% for Iran. When the global media system converges on a narrative, it converges completely. When it fractures, it fractures along cultural fault lines.

Nearly half of all Ukraine coverage belongs to a single mega-cluster. 49.8% of articles frame the invasion identically: unprovoked Russian aggression against a sovereign state. No equivalent dominant narrative exists for Iran, where the largest cluster captures only 18.5% of coverage. The world agreed on Ukraine; it fractured on Iran.

02
03

The moral clarity of the target determines framing consensus, not just the nature of the attacker. Ukraine is perceived as an unambiguous victim: democratic, European, no proxy networks. Iran carries moral baggage—Hezbollah, Hamas, nuclear ambiguity—that forces every outlet to adjudicate competing narratives. Epistemic fragmentation tracks moral complexity.

Dissent is distributed in Iran coverage but concentrated in Ukraine coverage. Arabic, Indonesian, German, and Korean media each dissent differently on Iran. On Ukraine, Russian-language media is the sole dissenting pole against near-universal condemnation. Multipolar disagreement versus bipolar disagreement.

04
05

Actions by hegemonic powers generate more epistemically diverse global reactions. US allies must negotiate their own complicity when the US strikes—France, Turkey, Indonesia each find different positions. When Russia invades, condemnation costs nothing. The hegemon's allies are forced to articulate positions; the adversary's critics face no such dilemma.

What a corpus cannot say reveals the boundaries of its epistemic horizon. Iran generated religious legitimacy and China-silence clusters absent from Ukraine. Ukraine generated Western-hypocrisy and military-resistance clusters absent from Iran. Structural absences are as diagnostic as presences.

Critical Finding

The 4× difference in contestation rates (57.7% vs 14%) is not a methodological artifact. It reflects a genuine difference in how legible each conflict is to analytical frameworks. Ukraine's "illegal Russian aggression" narrative is so dominant that even models with different training data converge on it. Iran's framing landscape has no equivalent consensus.

Cross-Case Analysis:
Why the Difference?

The most striking structural finding is the contrast between monopolar and multipolar framing. But explaining why requires examining three interacting factors that all push in the same direction.

The Three-Factor Model

FactorIran (fragmented)Ukraine (unified)
Moral clarity of the target Ambiguous—sovereign state and terror network sponsor. Hezbollah, Hamas, nuclear program create competing moral frames. Unambiguous—democratic sovereign victim. No proxy networks, no nuclear ambiguity. No "but they..." counterargument gains traction.
Hegemon accountability pressure US allies must negotiate complicity. France, Turkey, Indonesia each find different positions. The strike forces articulation. No complicity dilemma. Condemning Russia costs nothing for most nations. Consensus is the path of least resistance.
Nature of the military action Preemptive strike. "Was this proportional?" is inherently debatable. Legal ambiguity invites competing interpretations. Full-scale invasion of a sovereign state. Legality is unambiguous under international law. Less interpretive space.
Interpretive Note

All three factors push in the same direction, which explains the strength of the effect. Iran's moral ambiguity, the compliance pressure on US allies, and the legal debatability of preemptive strikes all generate framing diversity independently. Their convergence produces the 4× difference in epistemic fragmentation.

Methodology
Text extraction
trafilatura → newspaper3k → Wayback Machine. 86% success on 2022 URLs.
Translation
Helsinki-NLP MarianMT (20+ pairs) → Meta NLLB-200 (200 languages). All local.
Pass 1
Qwen3-32B. Open-ended framing prompt with no predefined categories. Country context injection for bias compensation (58 countries).
Council
Iran: full (3 models × 1,267). Ukraine: stratified sample (2 models × 307).
Clustering
LLM Pass 2, chunked hierarchical. Framing descriptions clustered in batches, merged across chunks.
Infrastructure
Qwen3-32B + Gemma-27B on 2× TITAN RTX (boron). Translation on RTX A4000 (nitrogen). All local inference, no cloud APIs.
On Methodology

No predefined framing categories were used at any stage. The LLM was asked to describe each article's framing in its own words. Clusters emerged from semantic similarity between these free-text descriptions. This means the categories above—"Justified Strategic Escalation," "Russian SMO Justification"—were discovered by the system, not imposed by researchers. The 76 Iran singletons and 68 Ukraine singletons represent articles that refused to cluster: each carries a unique epistemic position that no other article in the corpus shares.

This report was produced using the NewsKaleidoscope epistemic mapping system. Corpus data sourced from GDELT, World News API, and Reddit.
