The Same Week.
Two Different Wars.

In March 2026, US and Israeli forces struck Iran. 106 articles from 46 countries — newspapers, broadcasts, podcasts, and YouTube — described what happened. They did not describe the same event.

What America told itself — 6 sources
A justified response to nuclear threats and sovereignty violations. Emphasis on US preparedness, regime instability, and the moral reckoning of conflict.
What the world saw — 100 sources
A volatile mix of diplomatic efforts and military posturing. Strategic calculations, Iran's ambiguous intentions, and mediators like Oman trying to prevent escalation.
84% of framing language in US sources was self-referential — describing American actions, American readiness, American moral stakes. The rest of the world was watching something else entirely.
01 — Council Consensus

Three AI Models Read Every Article. They Agreed on Two.

We ran each article through three independent language models — Qwen 3, Gemma 3, and Mistral Small — and asked them to classify the framing. Out of 106 articles, only 2 produced unanimous agreement.

When three models trained on different data, by different teams, in different countries can only agree 1.9% of the time — the disagreement is the finding.

61 articles rated CONTESTED · 43 MEDIUM · 2 HIGH confidence

This isn't a flaw in the models. It's a measurement of how genuinely ambiguous geopolitical framing is. The same sentence, read by architectures trained on different slices of human text, produces different readings — because the framing itself is doing work that resists stable classification.

06 — Hidden Assumptions

87 Claims Smuggled in as Fact

A presupposition is a claim that a sentence treats as already settled — not argued for, just assumed. We extracted 87 of them across 15 high-ambiguity articles. The BBC alone contributed 10, several of which contradict each other within the same piece.

BBC (GB)
"President Donald Trump reiterated that Iran cannot be allowed to have a nuclear weapon"
Presupposes: Iran's nuclear program is inherently hostile and must be prevented at all costs.
Contested by: Iranian officials arguing their program is for civilian energy; JCPOA signatories who accepted enrichment under monitoring.
BBC (GB)
"More than 7,000 people have been killed by the Iranian regime in their crackdown against nationwide protests"
Presupposes: The Iranian government's internal violence delegitimizes its external claims.
Contested by: Iran, which frames internal security as sovereign; states with comparable protest responses who reject the linkage.
Press TV (IR)
"The Islamic Republic has consistently stated that its nuclear program is entirely peaceful"
Presupposes: Iran's declared peaceful intent is a settled fact, not a claim requiring evidence.
Contested by: IAEA reports documenting undeclared nuclear activities; Western intelligence assessments.
Al Jazeera (QA)
"Washington's military buildup in the Middle East has the region bracing for conflict"
Presupposes: US military presence is the cause of regional tension, not a response to it.
Contested by: US officials who frame the buildup as deterrence; analysts who cite Iranian proxies as the initial provocation.
The Hindu (IN)
"India's strategic autonomy requires balancing ties with both Washington and Tehran"
Presupposes: India is a neutral actor whose interests are served by non-alignment rather than taking sides.
Contested by: Analysts who argue India's energy dependence on Iran makes neutrality impossible; US officials seeking unambiguous alignment.
Dawn (PK)
"Pakistan's nuclear program has always been under strict civilian oversight and IAEA cooperation"
Presupposes: Pakistan's nuclear governance is a settled success story, not an ongoing concern.
Contested by: Proliferation researchers citing A.Q. Khan network history; Indian security analysts.

The BBC finding is striking: 10 presuppositions in a single article, several structurally contradictory. The same piece assumes both that Iran is an irrational actor and that deterrence (which requires rational calculation) will work. This isn't bias — it's the texture of how complex events get compressed into narrative.

09 — Method

How This Was Made

106 articles were collected via GDELT API, curated RSS feeds, YouTube channels, and podcast RSS from outlets across 46 countries. Non-English articles (42 of 106, in 18 languages) were translated using Helsinki-NLP and NLLB-200 models running locally. 11 oral sources — 9 YouTube broadcasts and 2 podcast episodes — were transcribed using faster-whisper large-v3 on GPU, adding 222 minutes of audio analysis to the corpus.

Each article was analyzed by a three-model LLM council — Qwen 3 32B (Alibaba), Gemma 3 27B (Google), and Mistral Small 3.1 24B (Mistral) — running sequentially on two TITAN RTX GPUs via llama.cpp. Models were given no predefined categories. Clustering emerged from the corpus, not from the researchers.

Presupposition extraction used targeted analysis of 15 high-ambiguity articles selected by council disagreement scores. Vocabulary asymmetry was measured across 486 actor-framing instances. Quote attribution was counted from syntactic feature extraction.

No external APIs were used. All inference ran on local hardware. The source corpus, analytical outputs, and council verdicts are preserved as an immutable research record.
