This is not a claim about what is true. It is a measurement of whose voice was treated as the default record of events.

The asymmetry goes further than source selection. Of 188 articles analyzed for syntactic structure, 73 displayed measurable tokenism — the practice of acknowledging a counter-argument in one clause while devoting multiple paragraphs, named sources, and causal evidence to the dominant frame. Forty-five of those cases were flagged as severe: an elaboration ratio exceeding 8 to 1. The average elaboration ratio across all analyzed articles was 42.3 to 1.

The position is no longer in the words. It is in the architecture of whose words get buildings and whose get doorways.

Measured at 42:1 average elaboration ratio. An article passes a surface audit for "balance" while encoding a dominant frame through structure alone.

Then there is what the pipeline called the consensus problem. Three independent language models — Qwen3:32B, Gemma-3-27B, and Mistral-Small — read each article and attempted to agree on its epistemic position. They agreed fully on just 2 of 94 articles. On 55 of them — 58.5 percent of the corpus — all three models reached different conclusions. This is not a calibration failure. It means that epistemic position in political journalism is not a stable property of a text. It is a reading produced by the interaction between text and interpreter. Every automated bias tool currently deployed assumes otherwise.

Five Positions

How the World Split

The corpus did not produce a spectrum from "pro-Iran" to "pro-US." It produced five distinct epistemic positions — each internally coherent, each reflecting a different set of assumptions about who has legitimate authority, what counts as aggression, and whose history is the relevant backdrop.

Thirty-six articles resisted classification. They were not centrist. They occupied a different register entirely — what this analysis calls technical-strategic: the presentation of political assumptions as technical facts. More on that below.

Vocabulary Asymmetry

What Words Do to Actors

The same military action, described in different words, produces different moral universes. The pipeline tracked how every major actor was characterized across the full corpus — which sanitizing terms (strike, operation, response) and condemnatory terms (attack, aggression, bombing) attached to which actors, and whether the ratio was consistent.

It was not consistent. Iran receives a condemnatory-to-sanitizing ratio of approximately 1.3 to 1. The US receives a ratio of 1.1 to 1. The IDF receives a ratio of 0.5 to 1 — more sanitizing than condemnatory. These are aggregate numbers across the full corpus including hostile sources. The gap is meaningful.

Framing score: negative = more condemnatory language applied to this actor; positive = more sanitizing language. Measured across 94 articles. Score reflects aggregate characterization across all sources — including hostile ones. The asymmetry persists after controlling for source alignment.

The most consequential vocabulary asymmetry is not in the framing scores. It is in the question that organizes the article. An ABC News analysis led with: "Will the US strike Iran?" — not "What happens to Iran if the US strikes?" The subject of history is the decision-maker. Iran exists as an object of that decision. Nothing needs to be asserted. The asymmetry lives in what is treated as real versus what is treated as speculative.

Presuppositional Framing

What Goes Without Saying

The hardest bias to detect is embedded in noun phrases — claims presented as background fact requiring no argument. The carrier phrase encodes the position before any assertion is made. A reader must actively resist the presupposition to notice it. Most readers do not.

The neutrality is the ideology. Western press does not perform objectivity. It performs technocracy — the presentation of political assumptions as technical facts. This is harder to read precisely because it is more sophisticated concealment.

Of 94 articles, 81 percent of those that resisted political classification were operating in what we call technical-strategic register: military assets, casualty ratios, strike coordinates, alliance dynamics. No moral claim stated. No political position taken — except in the choice of whose actions require justification, whose casualties are named, whose question organizes the piece.

The "view from nowhere" does not exist. What looks like it, under analysis, dissolves into a view from inside the assumptions of the actor whose actions require the least justification. In the Iran corpus, that actor is the United States.

Coverage Geography

45 Countries. What 20 Missing Languages Mean.

The coverage map shows where the pipeline reached. It does not show where opinion exists. Those are different maps, and the gap between them is one of the most important findings of this analysis.

Gold = covered. Dark = absent. Each country scaled by article count, not area.

The Middle East — the region most directly struck — had the lowest coverage ratio in this corpus. Arabic-language press largely sits behind paywalls or requires authentication. Iranian domestic press (Kayhan, ISNA, Shargh) was entirely absent: not because Iran did not cover its own bombardment, but because the pipeline had not yet built Persian RSS ingestion at time of this run. That is a pipeline failure, not a silence, and it will be corrected.

Southeast Asia — Indonesia (world's largest Muslim population), Malaysia, Vietnam, Thailand — present in source metadata but zero full-text articles retrieved. The framing exists. The pipeline could not reach it. Latin America contributed four articles from two countries for 650 million people. Central Asia, where Russian-language state press covers the event extensively, was not attempted.

What this means for every finding in this report: it is biased toward English, toward Western-aligned wire services, toward outlets legible to automated discovery. The five clusters are not a map of global opinion. They are a map of what is currently accessible to an automated English-centric pipeline. That is a meaningful result in itself — it maps the structure of global media legibility — but it must not be mistaken for completeness.

Absence as Data

What Nobody Said

The corpus has two kinds of silences. One is absence of coverage — regions and languages that weren't reached. The other is deeper: positions that are structurally unspeakable in legible media, regardless of language or geography. Absence of the second kind is not a gap in the pipeline. It is a finding about what global media collectively refuses to articulate.

Iran's nuclear program as legitimate self-defense
No outlet in the corpus — including Iranian state media — framed Iran's nuclear development as a rational response to documented US-Israeli capacity for regime change. This position is held by significant portions of the Iranian political class and the global non-aligned movement. It does not appear.
US presence in the region as structurally colonial
The framing of five US military bases within strike range of Iran as colonial infrastructure — analogous to how British basing in the 1950s was described at the time — does not appear in any article, including Al Jazeera and Press TV. The vocabulary of colonialism applies to past events; it is not applied to current US military geography.
Israeli nuclear capability as regional destabilizer
Israel's undeclared nuclear arsenal — estimated at 80–400 warheads, not subject to IAEA inspection, acknowledged by no formal treaty — does not appear as context in any article framing Iran's program as an "existential threat." The asymmetry in inspection regimes is structurally unmentionable in Western media and absent from most Global South coverage as well.
Iranian civilian population as a subject
The people who would live inside a bombed country appear in five articles, in one quote each. Civilians are not absent from the corpus because nothing happened to them. They are absent because the organizing question of Western coverage — "will the US strike?" — positions Iranians as the object of a decision being made elsewhere.

These are not fringe positions. They are held by significant portions of the world's population and routinely articulate in oral and semi-public discourse — in mosques, in vernacular YouTube, in political organizing — that automated analysis cannot yet reach. The pipeline's Tier 3 expansion (Friday sermons, podcast ingestion, Telegram channel monitoring) is designed specifically to surface what Tier 1 media structurally excludes. These findings will be revisited when that ingestion is complete.

Methodology

How This Was Built and What It Cannot Do

This report is honest about its own epistemic constraints. The system that produced it is not a neutral measurement instrument. It is an automated analysis pipeline with specific blind spots, which it attempts to document rather than conceal.

Corpus
94 articles, 45 countries, 18 languages. Primarily English-language (64 articles, 68%). February 26 – March 4, 2026.
Ingestion
GDELT API (gap-fill), curated RSS feeds, UN Security Council transcripts (emergency session). No paywall access. 20 articles skipped due to access barriers.
Analysis
Three-model LLM council (Qwen3:32B + Gemma-3-27B + Mistral-Small-3.1-24B). Two-pass clustering: free-form framing extraction, then emergent grouping. No pre-defined categories imposed.
Limitations
English-centric. No Persian, Arabic, or Urdu domestic press. Tier 3 sources (sermons, podcasts, Telegram) not yet ingested. Qwen anchoring effect documented but not yet corrected.
Confidence
58.5% of articles produced no three-model consensus. Findings presented with explicit uncertainty. Database is immutable — all runs preserved for comparison.
What this is not
A bias rating. A claim about truth. A complete map of global opinion. It is a map of what automated English-centric analysis can reach — and the shape of what it cannot.

Primary data sources: GDELT Event Database, UN Security Council official transcripts (sc16307), BLS, FRED, World Bank, ACLED. Source code and database schema available on request. All findings from a single event corpus — validation across future events is ongoing.
